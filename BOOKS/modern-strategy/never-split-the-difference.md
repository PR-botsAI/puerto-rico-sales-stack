# Never Split the Difference

**Author:** Chris Voss (with Tahl Raz)  
**Full title:** *Never Split the Difference: Negotiating As If Your Life Depended On It*  
**Published:** 2016  
**Era:** Modern strategy — the FBI-grade negotiation playbook applied to business and sales

---

## Why It Matters

Voss spent decades as the FBI's lead international kidnapping negotiator. He negotiated with terrorists, bank robbers, and hostage-takers in moments where the *literal cost of failure was a human life.* He then taught that methodology at Harvard and translated it for business.

The core insight: **traditional rational-actor negotiation is wrong about how humans actually decide.** Voss's methodology starts from the truth that humans are emotional, irrational, and reactive — and uses that to reach better outcomes for both sides.

---

## Core Thesis

**Tactical empathy** — the deliberate, professional understanding and acknowledgment of the other person's emotional state — is the master skill. Combined with a small kit of language tools, it produces better negotiated outcomes than logic-and-numbers approaches ever do.

## The Voss Tools

### 1. Mirroring
Repeat the last 1–3 words the other person said, with a slight upward inflection — a soft question.

- *Them:* "It's just too expensive for us right now."
- *You:* "Too expensive…?"
- *Them:* (fills the silence, often with the real objection)

Mirrors trigger people to elaborate, reveal more, and feel listened to. They cost nothing.

### 2. Labeling
Name the emotion you observe in the other person, hypothetically.

- *"It seems like you're worried about the timing."*
- *"It sounds like cost is the main concern."*
- *"It looks like you've had a bad experience with a vendor in this category before."*

Labels acknowledge the emotion, dissipating it. **Negative emotions named lose their grip.** Positive emotions named are reinforced.

**Critical:** never use *"I feel"* or *"I think."* Always *"it seems…"* / *"it sounds…"* / *"it looks…"* — framing it as observation, not assumption.

### 3. The Late-Night FM DJ Voice
Voss's signature: a low, calm, slow voice. Slower than feels natural. Slightly downward inflection. It signals authority, presence, control.

Use this voice in moments of tension. It is itself a state-management tool — for both you *and* the other party.

### 4. "That's Right" vs "You're Right"
*"You're right"* is what people say to end a conversation they want to escape. It means nothing.

*"That's right"* is what people say when they feel **truly understood.** It signals a breakthrough.

The goal of every Voss-style discovery: get the other party to say *"that's right."* From there, agreement on terms is achievable.

### 5. The Accusation Audit
Before the other party can voice their concerns, you voice them first — worse than they would.

- *"You're probably thinking I'm just another salesman who's going to push something on you that you don't need. You might be wondering whether the quote we sent is the real number or whether there's going to be a surprise. You might even be a little frustrated that we took 24 hours to follow up."*

By preempting and exaggerating the worst-case interpretations, you defuse them. The other party often *minimizes* the concerns you raised.

### 6. Calibrated Questions — "How?" and "What?"
Never ask *"why?"* (it triggers defensiveness). Ask *how* and *what* questions that put the burden of problem-solving on the other side.

- *"How am I supposed to do that?"* (a perfect, non-confrontational *no*)
- *"What would need to be true for this to work?"*
- *"How would your team see this?"*
- *"What's the biggest challenge you're facing right now?"*

Calibrated questions force the other party to consider your constraints, articulate their own needs, and frequently solve their own objections.

### 7. The No-Oriented Question
Most sales scripts chase *yes.* Voss flips it: ask questions designed to elicit *no.*

- *"Is this a bad time to talk?"* > *"Do you have a minute?"*
- *"Have you given up on solving this?"* (almost always: "No, we haven't.")
- *"Would it be a terrible idea if I sent over a one-pager?"*

Why? *No* feels safer than *yes.* People who say *no* feel in control, and people who feel in control engage more openly. *Yes* without commitment is worthless; *no* almost always invites the real conversation.

### 8. The Bending of Reality — Anchoring
Numbers are sticky. The first specific number anchors the negotiation.

- **Extreme anchor:** state a number much higher than your real target. Pulls the midpoint your way.
- **Range:** offering a range ("between $40k and $55k") makes the high end of your range feel reasonable.
- **Odd numbers:** $37,263 is read as *the result of careful calculation.* $40,000 is read as *negotiable opening.* Use odd numbers when you want to signal precision and finality.

### 9. The Ackerman System
Voss's structured offer ladder for a buyer:

1. Set a target price (your *real* goal).
2. First offer: **65%** of target.
3. Second offer: **85%** of target.
4. Third offer: **95%** of target.
5. Final offer: **100%** of target — with a non-monetary throw-in to signal you've truly reached your limit.

Use late-night DJ voice, calibrated questions, and labels between each step.

### 10. The Black Swan
In every deal there are 2–3 pieces of information you don't know that, if known, would change everything. Voss calls them Black Swans. Top negotiators hunt for them with calibrated questions, in-person observation, and listening 80% of the time.

Example: a buyer's apparent objection (*"price too high"*) often hides a Black Swan (*"I personally get evaluated on cost reduction this quarter and need a visible win"*). Find that Black Swan; restructure the deal around it.

## PR Application Notes

- **Mirroring is culturally easy in PR.** Conversation in Spanish often *naturally* includes repetition for emphasis (*"¿Muy caro? ¿Muy caro cómo?"*). Use it.
- **Labeling translates beautifully.** *"Parece que la preocupación principal es el pago mensual."* / *"Suena como que la familia tiene que verlo primero."* These soften the conversation and surface truth.
- **"That's right" — *"Exacto"* / *"Así mismo"*** is the breakthrough phrase to listen for.
- **The Accusation Audit** is powerful in PR because skepticism toward salespeople is high. Naming it first defuses it instantly.
- **No-oriented questions** dodge the cultural politeness that makes Boricua *"sí"* unreliable as a buying signal. A *"no"* in PR is a real *no.* A *"sí"* sometimes means *"I'm being polite while I think about it."*

## Action Items

- [ ] Build 3 labels for your most common prospect objections. Practice them out loud until they sound natural.
- [ ] Replace every *"do you…?"* opener with a *"have you…?"* or *"is there a reason not to…?"* this week. Note the response difference.
- [ ] On your next stalled deal, run an Accusation Audit before re-engaging. Then ask three calibrated *how/what* questions. No pitching for 10 minutes.
