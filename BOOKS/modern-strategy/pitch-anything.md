# Pitch Anything

**Author:** Oren Klaff  
**Full title:** *Pitch Anything: An Innovative Method for Presenting, Persuading, and Winning the Deal*  
**Published:** 2011  
**Era:** Modern strategy — frame control for high-stakes presentations

---

## Why It Matters

Klaff is an investment banker who has pitched billions in deals. He took the linguistics of frame control, the neuroscience of attention, and the rhythm of high-stakes finance presentations and produced a methodology nicknamed **STRONG.**

Where Voss is the negotiator's bible, *Pitch Anything* is the **presenter's** bible — the playbook for the moment when you have a room (or a single decision-maker) and a limited window to land your case.

---

## Core Thesis

Every interaction is a **collision of frames.** A frame is the mental container through which someone interprets a situation. In any meeting, one frame absorbs the other. The seller's job is to **enter, control, and hold** the dominant frame for the duration of the conversation.

The brain that processes your pitch is **not the analytical neocortex** — it is the **crocodile brain (croc brain),** an attention-filtering, threat-scanning, novelty-seeking organ that decides in seconds whether to engage or dismiss. Pitch to the croc brain first; the analytical brain comes second.

---

## The Five Frames You'll Encounter

### Hostile Frames (to break)
1. **The Power Frame** — the prospect signals dominance: "I only have 10 minutes," interrupts, checks the phone. Designed to make you the petitioner.
2. **The Time Frame** — weaponized scarcity of attention. "Let's wrap this up."
3. **The Analyst Frame** — deflects warmth and momentum by drilling into spreadsheet detail.

### Friendly Frames (to deploy)
4. **The Prize Frame** — *you* are the prize. The prospect must qualify for the privilege of working with you.
5. **The Intrigue Frame** — a partial story, a half-revealed insight, a pattern interrupt that keeps the croc brain hooked.
6. **The Moral Authority Frame** — your position rests on a principle bigger than the deal.

## Frame Breaks (Klaff's signature moves)

When the prospect throws a power frame, you respond with a **denial or a reframe** — calm, brief, slightly playful.

- *"Only ten minutes? Let's not waste either of ours. I'll spend two minutes on what matters and the rest is up to you."*
- *"Let me stop you there — we need to be sure this is even a fit before we go into the financials."*
- *"I get it. I have one other meeting today and I'm choosing my next one carefully too."*

The frame break must be:
- **Brief.** Long defenses are weak. One sentence; move on.
- **Calm.** No emotion bleeds through.
- **Slightly humorous or charged.** Engages the croc brain.
- **Followed by forward motion.** You don't dwell on the break — you advance the agenda.

## The STRONG Method

**S** — **Set the frame**  
Walk in with the prize/intrigue/moral-authority frame in place. Body language, voice, opening line.

**T** — **Tell the story**  
Pitches that *tell a story* hold attention. Pitches that *list features* lose attention in 90 seconds.

**R** — **Reveal the intrigue**  
A partial reveal of why this opportunity is interesting *now,* with one detail held back. Croc brain stays on the line waiting for the rest.

**O** — **Offer the prize**  
Flip qualification. *They* should be working to convince *you* this is a fit.

**N** — **Nail the hookpoint**  
The moment they *visibly* want what you have. You see it in their posture, their questions, their body. Identify it; capture it.

**G** — **Get the decision**  
Drive to a *specific next step.* Vague forward motion is failure. *"Let's regroup"* is failure. "What's the deadline for kicking this off?" is success.

## The 20-Minute Pitch Architecture

Klaff's hard rule: **the entire pitch is 20 minutes, no exceptions.** Beyond that, attention collapses.

The budget:
- **5 minutes** — Introduce yourself + the big idea.
- **10 minutes** — Explain the budget, the secret sauce, the why-now.
- **2 minutes** — Offer the deal.
- **3 minutes** — Stacking frames + answering questions while holding the frame.

Long pitches lose. Always.

---

## The Why-Now Forces

Klaff requires every pitch to articulate three forces that make the moment *now* (not next quarter) the right time:

1. **Economic forces** — macro/market conditions.
2. **Social forces** — changes in customer behavior / culture.
3. **Technological forces** — new capabilities, platforms, threats.

Without a credible *why-now,* the prospect defers. Defer = no deal.

---

## Avoiding Neediness

Klaff's most-quoted principle: **neediness is the kiss of death.** The croc brain detects it instantly and rejects the pitch.

Neediness signals:
- Re-asking the same question multiple ways.
- Following up *too often.*
- Talking past natural pause points.
- Apologizing for time or for the ask.
- *"What can I do to earn your business?"* (terminal neediness)

The antidote: a full pipe (Blount's discipline) so no single deal matters.

## PR Application Notes

- **Frame control works in any language**, but the *signals* of frame shift in PR. A long Boricua greeting is not weakness — it's the cultural setup. Patience through that setup *then* a confident frame is the move.
- **The Prize Frame** is less culturally familiar in PR. Apply it lightly. *"Estamos seleccionando con cuidado a quién le brindamos este programa"* is the right register — confident, respectful, *not* arrogant.
- **Storytelling beats data in PR even more than in the US.** A real customer story — named, specific, local — holds attention longer than any deck.
- **Why-now** must be locally framed. *Ley 22/60* sunsets, manufacturer-program endings, hurricane-season prep windows, school-year deadlines — PR-specific time pressures land hardest.
