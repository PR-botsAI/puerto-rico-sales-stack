# How to Master the Art of Selling

**Author:** Tom Hopkins  
**First published:** 1980  
**Era:** Foundational / Late classical — the closing-techniques canon

---

## Why It Matters

Hopkins is the high priest of the **trial close**. He was a 19-year-old failed real estate agent who, after intense training, sold more residential real estate than any human in his market — *in his early twenties.* This book is the playbook.

If you sell in person, on the phone, or anywhere a *commitment* must be earned in conversation, you read Hopkins.

---

## Core Thesis

Selling is a learnable, mechanical skill stacked on top of a relational foundation. You earn trust, you discover need, **then you use precise verbal techniques to guide the prospect to their own decision.** The trial close is the engine that drives the process.

---

## The Hopkins Framework

### 1. The Champion's Creed
Hopkins frames selling as professional athletics. Daily practice, drilling lines, role-play, mastery of the small mechanics. The amateur improvises; the pro rehearses.

### 2. Words That Sell vs Words That Repel

Hopkins introduced the *vocabulary swap* most modern sales trainers still teach.

| Replace this | With this |
|---|---|
| Cost / price | Investment |
| Down payment | Initial investment |
| Monthly payment | Monthly investment |
| Contract | Agreement / paperwork |
| Sign | Approve / authorize / OK |
| Sell / sold | Get involved / get them happily involved |
| Deal | Opportunity |
| Objection | Area of concern |
| Problem | Challenge |
| Pitch | Presentation |
| Commission | Fee for service |
| Customers | People we serve |

### 3. The Trial Close (Hopkins' signature)

A **trial close** is a question that probes for the close *without asking for the sale*. It tests temperature.

Examples:
- *"By the way, what color did you think would look best?"*
- *"Would weekday or weekend delivery work better for you?"*
- *"Is the warranty important to you?"*

If they answer affirmatively, you are *closer* to the close. If they hesitate, you've surfaced an objection cheaply.

### 4. The Tie-Down

A short statement-plus-question that earns micro-agreements.

- *"This feature saves you 30 minutes a day, **doesn't it?**"*
- *"Reliability matters when your family is in the car, **wouldn't you agree?**"*
- *"The lower fuel cost would make a real difference each month, **right?**"*

Tie-downs stack "yeses" — a direct application of Carnegie's principle #5 in Part 3.

### 5. The Alternate Advance (Choice Close)

Never ask *if* they want to proceed. Ask *how.*

- *"Would you prefer the white or the silver?"*
- *"Would you like to start with the basic plan or go straight to the premium?"*
- *"Cash, credit, or financing?"*

### 6. The Porcupine

When the prospect asks a question, *toss it back* before answering.

- Prospect: *"Does this come in blue?"*
- You: *"Would you want it in blue?"*
- (If yes, that's a buying signal. If no, you've avoided a wasted feature pitch.)

### 7. Hopkins' Objection Sequence

1. Hear them out fully.
2. Feed it back: *"So if I understand correctly…"*
3. Question it: *"Why do you feel that way?"*
4. Answer it.
5. **Trial close** on the resolution: *"Does that address your concern?"*
6. Move forward.

### 8. The Closes

Hopkins catalogs dozens. The greatest hits:

- **Assumptive Close** — act as if the sale is already happening. Start filling out the paperwork.
- **Sharp-Angle Close** — prospect asks for something extra; you trade: *"If I could get that for you, would you be ready to move forward today?"*
- **Ben Franklin Close** — draw the line down the page; benefits on the left, concerns on the right; let them see the math.
- **Reduction-to-the-Ridiculous Close** — break price into daily cost. *"Less than a cup of coffee a day."*
- **Lost Sale Close** — you appear to give up, ask what *you* did wrong; prospect lowers defenses, the real objection surfaces, you handle it, you close.
- **Order-Sheet Close** — start writing during the presentation; the form itself becomes the close.

## PR Application Notes

- **The vocabulary swap works in Spanish.** *Inversión* not *costo*. *Autorizar* not *firmar*. *Área de preocupación* not *objeción*. Drill these.
- **The trial close fits PR conversational rhythm.** Boricuas converse first; the soft tie-down (*"¿verdad?"*, *"¿usted no cree?"*) feels native rather than imported.
- **The Ben Franklin close** plays well because it respects the prospect's intelligence and the family-decision dynamic — the list goes home and gets reviewed.

---

## Where Hopkins Shows Up Elsewhere

- **Ziglar's** closes overlap heavily; the two were friendly contemporaries.
- **Cardone's** assumptive style is Hopkins on stimulants.
- **Hormozi's** *risk reversal* is the modern descendant of the Sharp-Angle.
- **Voss** would dispute the trial close — he prefers calibrated questions — but both schools agree: never ask a yes/no question you don't already know the answer to.
